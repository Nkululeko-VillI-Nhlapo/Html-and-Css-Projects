What role do we play as human beings to Climate Change and What are the Consequences

Human actions on Earth are changing the natural greenhouse. The burning of fossil fuels such as coal and oil has raised the concentration of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the atmosphere over the last century, this occurs because the combustion of oil produces CO2 by combining carbon in the air with oxygen (Nasa, 2021). The clearing of land for agriculture, industry, and other human activities has raised greenhouse gas concentrations to a lesser amount (Nasa, 2021), all this causes a global temperature to rise of which has unforgiving effects.

Consequences

  • Hotter temperatures would likely result in more evaporation and rainfall overall, although regional differences will exist, with some areas getting more rainfall and others becoming more dryer (Nasa,2021).
  • A strong greenhouse effect will warm the ocean and cause glaciers and ice sheets to partially melt, causing sea levels to rise. If the ocean heats, the water expands, adding to the rise in sea level (Nasa,2021).
  • Higher atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) levels can have both good and bad effects on crop production outside of a greenhouse. Plant development may be aided by increased CO2 levels, according to some laboratory research. Other effects, such as shifting temperatures, ozone, and water and nutrient scarcity, may, however, more than offset any potential yield improvement. If certain crops' ideal temperature ranges are exceeded, earlier production improvements may be decreased or reversed entirely (Nasa, 2021).
  • Finally, while increased CO2 stimulates plant growth, research has revealed that it decreases the nutritional content of most food crops by lowering protein and critical mineral concentrations in most plant species (Nasa, 2021). New pest and disease patterns may evolve as a result of climate change, impacting plants, animals, and humans and creating new dangers to food security, food safety, and human health (Nasa, 2021).

In the previous 151 years, the industrial activities that underpin our contemporary civilization have increased atmospheric carbon dioxide levels from 280 parts per million to around 417 parts per million. The panel also found that there is a high probability that human-produced greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide are likely the cause of much of the observed increase in Earth's temperatures during the past 50 years (Nasa, 2021).
